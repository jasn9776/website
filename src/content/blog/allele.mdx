## Starting with the wrong question

The original brief was: *"build a dashboard so researchers can explore allele frequencies in our Pacific cohort."* The data was already collected. The analysis was done. We just needed a way to look at it.

I spent the first two weeks building exactly that — a clean, fast interface for filtering by population, gene, and variant type. It worked. It was also, I realised, almost entirely useless to the people whose data it contained.

## A different kind of design meeting

The community engagement team set up a series of conversations with representatives from the participating communities. I went along mostly to listen.

What I heard: people wanted to be able to look up their own variants. They wanted to understand what the numbers meant without a genomics PhD. They wanted to know *who* could access their community's data, and they wanted that to be visible in the interface, not buried in a policy document.

None of that was in the original spec.

## What changed

**Contextual explanation over raw numbers.** Allele frequencies are shown alongside plain-language descriptions of what they mean in population terms. A frequency of 0.08 is not just 0.08 — it's "found in about 1 in 12 people in this population."

**Visibility of access controls.** Each dataset now shows a clear provenance tag indicating which community contributed it and what access restrictions apply. Researchers see this at point of query, not after.

**A community review layer.** Before any new dataset is added to the viewer, a designated contact at the contributing community can preview how it will be displayed. This added a few weeks to the release timeline. It was worth it.

## Technical notes

The stack is SvelteKit on the frontend, with a PostgREST API sitting in front of a PostgreSQL database. The row-level security policies in Postgres handle the access control — each dataset has a set of roles, and the API enforces them without any application-layer logic.

Search uses pg_trgm for fuzzy gene name matching, which turned out to be fast enough that we didn't need Elasticsearch.

## What I'd do differently

Start the community engagement before any code is written. Not after the first prototype, not halfway through — before. The design decisions that would have been cheap at week zero cost us significant refactoring at week six.

The interface is still primarily English. Supporting te reo Māori and Samoan is on the roadmap; it wasn't achievable in the initial timeline, and that's a genuine gap.
