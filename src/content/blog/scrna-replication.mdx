## The motivation

Could a frontier AI model meaningfully assist in replicating a scientific paper? If so, it would help in two ways: verifying the claims in the torrent of published research, and lowering the barrier for researchers without strong computational backgrounds to implement protocols from other labs.

The paper I picked was a study on CAR-T cell therapy available [here](https://www.doi.org/10.1016/j.xcrm.2023.101158). CAR-T is a treatment where a patient's own T cells are engineered to attack their cancer — remarkably effective for certain blood cancers, but with significant side effects. One of those is **prolonged cytopenia (PC)**: persistently low levels of blood cells (white, red, or platelets) after treatment. The paper I wanted to replicate investigated *why* some patients develop PC and others don't.

The main finding was that patients with PC had a "bone marrow infiltration of clonally expanded IFNγ-expressing CD8 T cells." The authors reached this conclusion through single-cell RNA sequencing of bone marrow samples from healthy donors and rrLBCL patients (a type of aggressive non-Hodgkin lymphoma that has relapsed or failed to respond to initial chemotherapy), comparing those with and without PC.

For simplicity, I focused on the single-cell RNA-seq analysis and skipped the flow cytometry validation experiments.


## Setting up the replication

I gave Claude two links — one to the paper and one to the publicly available dataset on CellxGene — and asked it to replicate the clustering and group comparisons. I used the **interview pattern**: rather than letting Claude guess at what I wanted, I asked it to question me on the specifics. It came back with a tight set of choices: which comparisons to focus on (PC vs no-PC? rrLBCL vs healthy? CD8 subclustering?), what language to use, and how faithfully to redo the upstream pipeline.

Within minutes of my answers, Claude had read the paper, understood the context, and produced a modular Python pipeline — four scripts covering download, clustering, composition analysis, and CD8 subclustering. What impressed me was that it did this **without being able to see the dataset itself**. When it didn't know what a column would be named, it wrote defensive code that tried common alternatives and printed a helpful error if none matched. It even recommended a specific Python version (3.11) based on the dependency compatibility of the packages involved.

## Part one: Running the code

Inevitably, the code didn't run perfectly the first time — Claude's sandbox doesn't have access to the actual data, so some errors only surfaced once I ran it locally. But the troubleshooting loop was fast. Each error I pasted back got a considered response: an explanation of the root cause, a fix, and often a link to the relevant GitHub issue or documentation page. Some examples:

- A scanpy/harmonypy version incompatibility causing a wrong-shape error → Claude identified it as a known issue, linked to the open scanpy GitHub ticket, and rewrote the call to be version-agnostic.
- A `KeyError` on gene symbols like `CD4` → Claude recognized this as the CellxGene convention of storing Ensembl IDs as the index with symbols in a separate column, and produced a one-block fix.
- A "data is already log-transformed" warning → Claude traced it to a stale `uns['log1p']` flag inherited from the parent AnnData object.

This was the first useful lesson: **Claude can debug errors in code it wrote without seeing the runtime environment, as long as you feed back the traceback**.

## Part two: Refinement of clustering and checking AI generated justifications

The initial clustering for all cells produced a similar number of clusters to the original paper (I obtained 22 as opposed to the paper's 26). 
When I uploaded the figure to claude (in image format), it was able to directly interpret the .png file and accurately described the produced plot from the analysis, showcasing the increased multi-modal capabilities of these models. 
When questioned as to how it obtained these cell markers, it said that it used the paper's own markers combined with standard scanpy/seurat tutorials and domain knowledge from training. 
When I googled each marker to check if it was a legitimate gene marker for each cell type, there seemed to be results from published literature that indicated they were indeed correct (so no obvious hallucinations).

## Part three: CD8 T-cell subclustering analysis

After replicating the main clustering, the paper also looked at the CD8 T-cells separately and subclustered them.  

Getting the code to run is a much weaker bar than getting it to produce the *right* answer. I was able to replicate the main finding — a CX3CR1ʰⁱ CD8 effector T cell population enriched in PC patients, with exactly the marker signature the paper reported (granzymes GZMA, GZMB, GZMH; FGFBP2; CX3CR1). But getting there wasn't a single clean run.

What surprised me was Claude's ability to **push me toward analyses to diagnose and troubleshoot issues**. After an initial clustering looked plausible, it suggested I check the per-patient composition of each cluster. That check revealed the CX3CR1ʰⁱ cluster was dominated by patient 7 (around 40% of cells) and patient 9. Was this a batch effect (Harmony failing to integrate), a contamination issue (NK cells being miscalled as CD8s), or actual biology (those two patients having genuinely clonally expanded T cells, as the paper describes)?

Claude proposed three concrete diagnostics to distinguish these, and walked me through interpreting the output. The AI's diagnosis: my initial CD8 identification was too loose and had let in
NK cells (TYROBP+, FCER1G+, KLRF1+) and γδ T cells (TRDC+), which share the cytotoxic expression program and cluster nearby. 
After tightening the cell-type detection and increasing Harmony's batch-correction strength, the contamination was resolved, 
and the patient 7/9 pattern partially persisted — exactly as the paper predicts for genuine clonal expansion.

After the refinements, I ended up with 7 sub-clusters of CD8 T-cells (compared to the original paper's 6). These clusters and along with key marker genes are below:

## Limits of what I could verify

Not everything was verifiable by me alone. While I have some domain knowledge in biology, I do not know if the cell markers identified by the AI are accurate (alhough a quick online search did not reveal any obvious errors).

I could verify:

- The marker genes Claude identified for identifying the type of each cluster seemed to be supported by literature
- The overall pipeline structure matched what the paper described in its methods: preprocessing, clustering, statistical analyses

I could not easily verify:

- Whether the slight differences in my results compared to the paper were do to with my undersampling (I only used a random 65% of the cells as I ran all analyses on my laptop with 16GB ram), 
differences in the algorithms (the paper used R/Seurat wheras I used Python/Scanpy) or differences in how the AI implemented the tests.
- whether the biological justifications made by the AI were completely accurate, such as key genes used to identify different cell types.
- whether the code was completely accurate and without errors
- whether some of the conclusions were accurate or AI hallucinations to achieve its goal of seeming to replicate the paper
- whether AI models are becoming more reliable or whether the hallucinations are just less obvious

What I chose not to try and replicate:
- The paper had a custom method for cell type annotation based on another dataset. For simnplisticity, I opted to just use known cell markers. This approach was sufficient to replicate the main finding.

what I didn't attempt:

- While the main logic was sound, certain details were implemented differently to the paper. For example in the paper it states 
"Analysis of the frequency of each cell cluster relative to all cells from each sample identified a single subset of CD8 T cells"
while the AI, as it seems for covenience, opted to use all CD8 cells instead of ALL Cells. This was probably the reason for patients 7 and 9 having a very large fraction of their cells being of the CX3CR1ʰⁱ clusters.
- I did not try to do this in an environment where the AI had direct access to the files (which would have prevented many errors). Currently Claude code only uses up to Opus 4.6.

## Takeaways

Three things stood out:

**Frontier AI can genuinely participate in scientific replication, not just assist with code.** Claude didn't just translate the paper's methods into Python — it noticed when results looked suspect, proposed diagnostic checks, interpreted the output in biological terms, and suggested fixes informed by knowledge of how bone marrow scRNA-seq typically fails (NK contamination, clonal patient dominance). That's a level of engagement that goes beyond code generation.

**The interview pattern is the right starting point for open-ended tasks.** Rather than specifying the project upfront, asking Claude to question me produced a scoped, prioritized plan that avoided wasted work on parts of the paper I didn't care about.

**Domain and technical/coding knowledge is still critical.** I was not too confident of the specific genes and cell classification steps because I lack that deep biological knowledge to verify that 
what the AI claimed was in fact correct. Whilst a quick google search seemed to confirm these markers, it still is an advanatage to have someone with deep knowledge of the field.
Notably, I did not observe obvious hallucinations — every gene, method, and library call I checked seemed correct. My coding background gave me a bit more confidence to trust and verify the AI output and ask for clarifications for things I was not sure were totally correct.
